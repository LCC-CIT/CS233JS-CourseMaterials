## Dice Games

Because the concentration game is structured to teach **state management**, **event handling (clicks)**, **delayed evaluation (`setTimeout`)**, and **DOM separation**, good dice-game assignments should hit those same concepts. 

Here are three dice games that perfectly mirror the structure of this card game and would allow students to use the newly refactored code as a direct template:

### 1. "Dice Memory" (Direct Translation)
**The Game:** Instead of a 20-card grid, the user sees a grid of 12 blank squares. Clicking a square "rolls" a hidden die (shows a face from 1-6). The user must find matching pairs of dice faces.
**Why it maps perfectly:** 
- **State (`gameLogic`):** Needs an array of 12 numbers (two of each 1-6) that get shuffled upon initialization. Needs `firstPick` and `secondPick`.
- **UI (`ui`):** Replaces `showCardFace` with `showDiceFace(index, value)`, swapping `cardjh.jpg` for `dice_4.png`. 
- **Flow:** Click -> Show Date -> Click -> Show Dice -> Disable Grid -> Wait 2 Seconds -> Match/Hide -> Enable Grid.
**Challenge for student:** Synthesizing the logic directly; adapting the `fillImages` function to fill numbers 1-6 twice instead of building string filenames from suits/ranks.

### 2. "Push Your Luck" / "Pig" (Accumulating State)
**The Game:** A single player rolls a die repeatedly. If they roll a 2-6, that number is added to their "Turn Total". They can click "Roll" or "Bank". If they choose "Bank", the Turn Total is added to their "Grand Score". If they roll a 1, their Turn Total becomes 0 and their turn automatically ends. The goal is to reach a Grand Score of 50.
**Why it maps perfectly:**
- **State (`gameLogic`):** Needs `turnTotal`, `grandScore`, and a `rollDice` function instead of a `pickCard` function. 
- **UI (`ui`):** Needs to update score displays and show the correct die face based on the random number generated by the logic.
- **Flow:** Click "Roll" -> check if 1. If 1 -> disable Roll button, `setTimeout` for 2 seconds to let the player see their failure, then reset turn. If 2-6 -> update scores wait for next click.
**Challenge for student:** Moving away from a grid of DOM elements to just one standard display element, and managing two different types of numbers (turn score vs. total score).

### 3. "Yahtzee-Lite" (Selective State Holding)
**The Game:** The user has 5 dice. On turn one, they click a "Roll All" button to generate 5 random dice. They can then click individual dice to "hold" them (changing their CSS border or opacity). They click "Roll Remaining" to re-roll the unheld dice. After 3 total rolls, the game checks if they have a Yahtzee (all 5 dice match) or a Full House (3 of one, 2 of another) and displays a win/loss message.
**Why it maps perfectly:**
- **State (`gameLogic`):** Needs an array for `diceValues` (length 5) and a boolean array for `heldDice` (length 5). Needs a `rollsLeft` counter.
- **UI (`ui`):** Needs `toggleHoldStyle(index)` which is similar to disabling a card. Needs to update dice images when rolled.
- **Flow:** The `handleClick` function on a die element tells the logic to toggle `heldDice[index]` instead of recording a first/second pick. The "Roll" button evaluates the state.
**Challenge for student:** Introducing a boolean state array to track which UI elements should respond to the next action, and writing a slightly more complex `isMatch()` equivalent (checking an array for Yahtzees/array for 5-of-a-kind).

Here are two excellent dice games that maintain the exact same architectural concepts (managing a board of elements, clicking items, delayed evaluation, and separating Logic from UI) but play very differently than a memory-match game:

### 1. "Make 7" (Dice Elimination)
**The Game:** 
A grid of 12 (or 20) dice is rolled randomly at the start of the game, displaying faces from 1 to 6. The player's goal is to clear the board. Instead of finding matching pairs, the player must click two dice whose faces **add up exactly to 7** (e.g., a 1 and a 6, or a 3 and a 4). 

**How it utilizes the refactored structure:**
*   **`gameLogic` (Model):** Remains almost identical, tracking an array of numbers, `firstPick`, and `secondPick`. The major educational shift is that the student must rewrite `fillImages()` to generate an array of random numbers 1-6 instead of pairs, and rewrite `isMatch()` to check if `val1 + val2 === 7`.
*   **`ui` (View):** Operates the same way—changing the appearance of a clicked die to show it is "selected", and hiding it when removed. 
*   **Controller:** The flow is identical: Click -> Record pick -> If second pick, disable board -> `setTimeout` -> Check `gameLogic.isMatch()` -> Either remove dice or deselect -> Enable board.
*   **Added Challenge:** Have the students add a "Reroll Remaining" button that costs a point/try, requiring them to add a new interaction that tells `gameLogic` to randomize non-removed dice and tells `ui` to update their images.

### 2. "Shut the Box" (Target Sum)
**The Game:**
The screen displays 9 clickable square tiles numbered 1 through 9. At the start of a turn, the computer "rolls" two dice (e.g., a 3 and a 5, for a total of 8). The player must click a combination of tiles that add up exactly to that target roll (e.g., clicking the '8' tile, or the '5' and '3' tiles, or the '1' and '7' tiles). Once the tiles match the target, they are "shut" (removed or disabled), new dice are rolled, and the game continues until the player shuts all 9 tiles.

**How it utilizes the refactored structure:**
*   **`gameLogic` (Model):** They will need an array storing the state of the 9 tiles (`true` for open, `false` for shut). Instead of `firstPick` and `secondPick`, they will need an array of `currentPicks` and a property for `currentTargetSum`.
*   **`ui` (View):** They will write DOM functions to format tiles as "Open", "Selected", or "Shut", and a function to update the two master dice images showing the current target roll.
*   **Controller:** `handleTileClick` tells the logic to add the tile to the current selection. The controller checks if the sum of the selected tiles equals the `currentTargetSum`. If it does, `setTimeout` for 1 second, tell the logic to "shut" those tiles, roll new dice, and update the UI. If the sum goes *over* the target, `setTimeout`, shake/flash the tiles red, and deselect them so the player can try again.

**Why these are great for students:**
Both games allow students to copy the `gameLogic` / `ui` / `Controller` skeleton directly. It forces them to look at *how* the data behaves (e.g. changing `===` to `+ === 7` or tracking a sum rather than exactly two picks) without getting lost in the DOM manipulation of the DOM, because the `ui` object abstracts the HTML away.

## Domino Games

Here are three domino games that perfectly map to the state-logic-UI architecture you've established in the concentration game, without being a simple "find the matching pair" memory variant:

### 1. "Domino Chain" (Sequential Matching)
**The Game:** 
A grid of 20 random dominos is shown face up. The player must clear the board by clicking dominos that form a valid chain. If they click a `[3|5]`, their next click *must* be a domino containing a `5` (e.g., `[5|2]`). If they click `[5|2]`, their next click must contain a `2`.
**How it uses the architecture:**
- **State (`gameLogic`):** `images` array holds the domino data (e.g., `'domino_3_5.png'`). `firstPick` tracks the currently "active" end of the chain.
- **UI (`ui`):** Very similar to the current code. The active domino is highlighted, valid subsequent clicks remove the old domino and highlight the new one.
- **Flow:** Click -> Check if `firstPick` is -1. If yes, it's the start of the chain; highlight it and set it as `firstPick`. If no, extract the numbers from the `firstPick` filename and the `newPick` filename. If one number matches, `removeCard(firstPick)`, set `firstPick = newPick`. If it doesn't match, `setTimeout` to flash red and keep the current `firstPick`.
**Educational value:** Teaches students to parse two pieces of data from a single string/object and check for overlapping values (`val1A === val2A || val1A === val2B`, etc.) rather than an exact string match.

### 2. "Double Trouble" (Target Sums by Type)
**The Game:**
A board of 15 dominos is shown. The goal is to clear the board. Players can click *one* "Double" domino (a domino where both sides are the same, like `[4|4]`) to remove it instantly, OR they can click *two* standard dominos whose total pip count adds up to 12. 
**How it uses the architecture:**
- **State (`gameLogic`):** Requires logic to determine if a single click resolves a turn, or if it must wait for a second click. 
- **UI (`ui`):** Identical abstraction—shows faces, hides them, disables clicks.
- **Flow:** Click -> if it's a Double, `removeCard` immediately and add to score. If it's a normal domino, set `firstPick`. On second click, sum all four pips across the two dominos. If sum === 12, remove both. If not, `setTimeout` for 2 seconds to show they don't match, then deselect.
**Educational value:** Introduces branching logic in the controller based on the *type* of data clicked. The student has to modify the `handleClick` flow so that it isn't strictly alternating between pick 1 and pick 2.

### 3. "Domino Drop / High-Low" (Comparative Elimination)
**The Game:**
A grid of face-down dominos is presented, plus one "Target" domino face-up at the top. The player clicks a face-down domino to reveal it. To remove it, its total pip count must be *strictly greater* than the Target domino. If it is, the clicked domino is removed, and the Target domino is updated to become the one they just clicked. If it's lower, the player loses a "life", the domino is flipped back face down, and the Target stays the same. 
**How it uses the architecture:**
- **State (`gameLogic`):** Tracks `currentTargetIndex` and `currentPick`. `tries` becomes `lives` (starting at 3 and counting down).
- **UI (`ui`):** Needs a special designated div for the Target, and the standard grid for the remaining dominos. 
- **Flow:** Click -> reveal domino -> calculate sum of pips vs Target pips. If higher -> `setTimeout` to let player see it, then remove it from grid, update Target UI to match it, update Target logic. If lower -> `setTimeout` to let player see failure, then flip back down and subtract a life.
**Educational value:** Moves away from equality (`===`) and focuses on comparative logic (`>`). It also requires managing a specific specialized piece of state (the Target) separate from the general grid array.



Here are two more domino game ideas that fit perfectly into the refactored logic/UI/controller architecture:

### 1. "Domino Mexican Train Lite" (Appending to a Shared Array)
**The Game:**
The player is given a "hand" of 7 face-up dominos. In the center of the screen is the "Train" (an empty array/row to start). A starting "Engine" domino (e.g., `[12|12]`) is placed in the train. On their turn, the player must click a domino from their hand that matches the *open end* of the Train. For example, if the Train ends in a `5`, they must click a domino containing a `5` (like `[5|8]`). If they do, it moves from their hand to the Train, and the new open end is `8`. If they have no playable dominos, they must click a "Draw" button to add a new domino to their hand. The goal is to empty their hand.

**How it uses the architecture:**
- **State (`gameLogic`):** Requires a `playerHand` array and a `train` array. Needs an `addDominoToTrain(handIndex)` function that checks if the ends match, and if so, moves the data from one array to the other.
- **UI (`ui`):** Instead of a static grid, the UI objects need functions to `renderHand(handArray)` and `renderTrain(trainArray)`. When a move is made, the UI re-draws the two areas based on the updated arrays.
- **Flow:** Click hand domino -> pass index to `gameLogic`. If `gameLogic.checkValidMove(index)` is true, move domino to train. Tell UI to redraw the hand and the train. No `setTimeout` needed, making the flow slightly simpler but the state manipulation more complex.

**Educational value:** This is an excellent way to teach array manipulation (using `push()`, `splice()`, or assigning nulls) and dynamic rendering. Instead of selectively hiding pre-rendered grid elements (`display: none`), they learn to empty a container `div` and rebuild its contents based on the state array.

### 2. "Boneyard Bluff" / "Higher-Lower Sequence"
**The Game:**
A single domino is dealt face up. Next to it is a face-down domino. The player must guess if the *total pip count* of the face-down domino is "Higher" or "Lower" than the face-up domino. They click a "High" or "Low" button. The face-down domino is revealed. If they are correct, they get a point, the old face-up domino is discarded, the revealed one slides over to become the new face-up domino, and a new face-down one is drawn from the "Boneyard" (deck). The goal is a streak of 10 correct guesses.

**How it uses the architecture:**
- **State (`gameLogic`):** Instead of a 20-item grid, the state holds the full shuffled boneyard (e.g., 28 dominos). It tracks `currentDomino` and `nextDomino` (popping them off the boneyard array). It needs an `evaluateGuess(isHighGuess)` function.
- **UI (`ui`):** Very focused: `showLeftDomino(filename)`, `showRightDominoBack()`, `showRightDominoFace(filename)`, and button click bindings.
- **Flow:** Click "High" button -> `gameLogic.evaluateGuess('high')`. Tell UI to reveal the right domino. `setTimeout` for 2 seconds. Update Score. Shift right domino data to left domino data in logic. Tell UI to update left domino image and show a blank back for the right domino. Wait for next click.

**Educational value:** This teaches managing a "deck" (popping items from an array so they aren't repeated), handling boolean logic based on dedicated control buttons rather than clicking the game pieces themselves. It heavily reinforces the `setTimeout` pattern to let the user see the result before resetting the board state resets.
